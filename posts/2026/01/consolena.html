Consolena

Vim Lena
January 4, 2026

Overview/Screenshots

Four days ago, I released Consolena v 1.0.0. This is something I have been working on slowly for quite a while to get it correct before it’s shipped and I’m happy to properly share it.

Consolena is a monospace typeface with a focus on legibility and consistency. They each come in regular-width and condensed-width versions that also come with or without ligatures.

Looks best at a slightly larger minimum size than most people seem to typically use; in my tests, about 12.5–13.5 pt depending on the platform/app. Many screens are very high resolution these days so you can still see a lot without straining your eyes. You can make your text slightly larger, I believe in you.

  • Consolena → Regular width, no ligatures
  • ConsolenaC → Condensed width, no ligatures
  • ConsolenaL → Regular width, includes ligatures
  • ConsolenaLC → Condensed width, includes ligatures

Notes

Consolena is based on Iosevka 33.3.6 by Belleve Invis, specifically the SS04 DejaVu Sans Mono/Meslo stylistic set with term spacing.

Consolena was created using the Iosevka Customizer Tool then tweaking the parameters/build plans file with some minor additional tweaks using the Glyphs app (which I highly recommend for type design).

A huge benefit of being based on Iosevka is that Consolena has support for Cyrillic and Greek characters. I do not speak Greek or any language that used these character sets, but the defaults for Iosevka SS04 are very sane so that’s nice.

Not everybody likes ligatures, which is why I made versions that do not have as well as versions that include ligatures. For versions that support ligatures, I include most common ligatures except for != → ≠ because I personally find that to be visually a bit disorienting.

My thoughts with regards to the specific letterforms I chose was to create something that I actually wanted to use. It looks nice and all the commonly used characters are distinct but it is still coherently styled. Meslo/ Menlo/ DejaVu Sans Mono are really iconic and well-loved coding typefaces which influenced my decision to use it as inspiration.

I wanted the letterforms to look similar in italics and roman versions, but for it to have elegant and distinctive italics. The italic slant is 9° which is slightly less severe than many italics.

The zero is not quite slashed or dotted, it’s basically a hybrid between the two. I like the distinctiveness of a dotted zero, but not how it looks. I like the look of a slashed zero, but don’t like how it can be confused with the empty set character (∅) or the slashed zero character (Ø or ø) common in some languages.
